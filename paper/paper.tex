\section{Summary}

\texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} (installed and imported as \texttt{my\_swamp}) is a Python
package for modeling the two-dimensional dynamics of exoplanet atmospheres. It
solves the shallow-water equations on the rotating sphere with a
spectral-transform core --- triangular truncation, Gauss--Legendre quadrature in
latitude, and fast Fourier transforms in longitude --- advancing absolute
vorticity, divergence, and a geopotential field forward in time
\citep{Hack:1992, Langton:2008}. The package is a \texttt{JAX} \citep{jax:2018}
reimplementation of \texttt{SWAMPE} \citep{Landgren:2022}, an established
NumPy/SciPy shallow-water model benchmarked for synchronously rotating hot
Jupiters and sub-Neptunes \citep{Landgren:2023}. \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} reproduces
the numerical results of \texttt{SWAMPE} to high precision, but expresses the
entire time integration as a differentiable, just-in-time-compiled program. As a
result, the simulated atmosphere --- its geopotential maps, wind fields, and
derived observables --- is an end-to-end differentiable function of the planet's
physical parameters and initial conditions, can be compiled to run on GPUs, and
can be vectorized across batches of parameter combinations in a single call.

Interpreting exoplanet atmospheres requires models that bridge the gap between
fast one-dimensional models \citep{Bell:2018}, which by
construction cannot resolve longitudinal structure, and three-dimensional
general circulation models, which capture the full spatial structure but are
computationally expensive --- a single model can cost hundreds to thousands of
core-hours, dominated by the coupled radiative transfer, and exploring a
parameter grid can take months \citep{Kataria:2016, Way:2017}. Two-dimensional
shallow-water models occupy a middle ground: they capture the
day--night circulation \citep{Perez-Becker:2013}, equatorial superrotation
\citep{Showman:2011}, and, in some regimes, atmospheric time-variability
\citep{Menou:2003} of tidally locked planets, while remaining cheap enough to
sweep parameter space. \texttt{SWAMPE} made this class of model openly available
and well documented in Python \citep{Landgren:2022, Landgren:2023}.

A growing set of methods, however, needs not just fast forward simulations but
their derivatives. Gradient-based optimization, inverse problems that fit
observed phase curves and eclipse maps, and the training of machine-learning
emulators all depend on the sensitivity of the simulated state to the underlying
physical controls --- the radiative and drag timescales, the forcing amplitude,
the reference geopotential, and the rotation rate. Estimating these sensitivities
by finite-differencing a NumPy model is slow and noisy, and the cost scales with
the number of parameters. \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} removes this bottleneck by
re-expressing the \texttt{SWAMPE} solver in \texttt{JAX}, so that exact gradients
are available through automatic differentiation of the full time integration at a
cost comparable to the forward simulation itself. Fitting a thermal phase curve
or secondary-eclipse map \citep{Knutson:2007, Rauscher:2018} to recover the
radiative and drag timescales and the day--night forcing amplitude then becomes a
single gradient-based optimization rather than a finite-difference loop whose
cost grows with the number of parameters.

\texttt{SWAMPE}, so that existing workflows port with minimal changes, while

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Differentiability.} The time-stepping loop runs inside
  \texttt{jax.lax.scan}, which makes the terminal state and any scalar loss
  differentiable with respect to continuous parameters (the day--night forcing
  contrast, radiative and drag timescales, mean geopotential, rotation rate,
  planetary radius, timestep, and hyperdiffusion coefficients) and with respect
  to explicit initial conditions. Reverse-mode gradients (\texttt{jax.grad}) are
  validated against centered finite differences; forward-mode (\texttt{jax.jvp},
  \texttt{jax.jacfwd}) differentiation is also supported. Because reverse-mode
  cost is independent of the number of parameters, the gradient of a scalar loss
  with respect to four physical parameters at once costs about $2.3\times$ a
  single forward integration (at spectral truncation $M=42$), versus the five
  forward runs a finite-difference estimate would require.
\item \textbf{Acceleration and vectorization.} The same code is just-in-time
  compiled with \texttt{jax.jit} and runs unchanged on CPU or GPU.
  \texttt{jax.vmap} maps the simulator over batches of parameters, enabling large
  ensemble sweeps and the generation of emulator training sets in a single
  compiled call.
\item \textbf{Numerical parity.} For default settings, \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX}
  reproduces \texttt{SWAMPE}'s spectral transforms, modified-Euler time
  differencing, Robert--Asselin filtering, sixth-order hyperdiffusion
  \citep{Gelb:2001}, and Newtonian-relaxation-plus-drag forcing, including the
  historical conventions of the reference code. A \texttt{pytest} regression
  suite enforces agreement against stored snapshots from the reference NumPy
  implementation to fixed absolute tolerances (better than $10^{-10}$ on
  vorticity and divergence and $5\times10^{-8}$ on the geopotential), and a
  long-run comparison reproduces the \citet{Williamson:1992} Test~2 steady
  balanced flow --- one of the two standard spherical shallow-water test cases
  included in \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} --- to a relative $L_2$ error below $10^{-11}$
  in every field over a ten-day integration (\autoref{fig:parity}).
\end{itemize}

These features let \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} serve both as a drop-in replacement for

\section{Relation to SWAMPE and similar tools}

\texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} is an independent reimplementation of \texttt{SWAMPE}
\citep{Landgren:2022}, not a wrapper around it: the two share an interface and a
set of physical benchmarks but no source code. Differentiable, GPU-capable
simulation has reshaped neighboring fields --- machine-learning-accelerated
computational fluid dynamics \citep{Kochkov:2021}, neural general circulation
models for Earth weather and climate \citep{Kochkov:2024}, and general spectral
frameworks such as Dedalus \citep{Burns:2020}. Within exoplanet science,
\texttt{ExoJAX} \citep{Kawahara:2022} brought automatic differentiation to
high-resolution radiative transfer and spectral retrieval; \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} is
complementary in that it makes the atmospheric \textit{dynamics} differentiable.
To our knowledge, no openly available exoplanet shallow-water model exposes
gradients through the full atmospheric integration, and \texttt{SWAMPE-JAX} fills
that gap for the exoplanet community while retaining the validated physics of
\texttt{SWAMPE}.
